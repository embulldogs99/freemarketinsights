ROA Shows Excessive Returns

I first discovered the excessive tendancies of High ROA - Return on Assets- Stocks
a few years ago reading Asness, Frazzini, and Pedersen's Quality Minus Junk

Return on Assets is a metric of how much Net Income is earned from an Asset Base ROA = NI / A

They found excess returns, and the work acts as one of the first complex, comprehensive,
and competitive stock models that attempts to incorperate most of the prior academic research.

However, they only use ROA as a single component of their theorized high quality stock.

The variable blending done in the paper causes a blocks visibility to the prominent drivers

As investors become aware and can incorperate the learnings and strategies present in
this paper, the market will adjust to incorperate the awareness of the new learnings,
but will still act irrationally around the weightings of the relevant variables

This behavior may cause return swings between variables where one Metric or Characteristic
becomes the flavor of the month/year/ect.
but the duration may be variable as well.

Considering these market behaviors, we like to look for sustained and consistent market returns
which may shed insight on factors that are overbought/overvalued or are just beginning to
become a fad.

I am continuing to gather data to conduct additional tests on ROA,
however, looking at monthly returns over the past two years,
we see a significant return differential between stocks selected with the model ( 1's )
and those that were not ( 0's )



Stocks with a 4 * ROA > 2 ---> ROA > 1/2

saw a 4 month annualized return of 32% while the rest of the market grew 22%.

This formula was actually even more optimized before I rounded up to generalize the concept
in attempts to not overly optimize the data set.

It may be interesting to note that the trend does not appear to persist in months 5 as the
data becomes old news.

The Asset and Net Income values were pulled at the fiscal year end for the SnP600 stocks and were held into the following year, implying the market took ~4 months to accurately rebalance funds
to reflect the new information.

This result is surprising for a 2017 world where technology should be making these moves occur
much more quickly.

To differentiate between a recent and now dead fad, or a persistent pattern is difficult.
Even with the grandest of datasets, there can still be questions of fad durations and current
market conditions never reacting like previous time periods.

However, I do enjoy retesting research I've read years ago that was published even many more year
before, and still finding replications of previous market observations.

Until Artifical Intelligence reaches financial markets and implements methodologies unimaginable
by a human mind, we will likely continue to: identify, attempt to capitalize on, snowball with other investors
and subsequently repeat trends that have happened before.

Concepts from Human Psychology and Game Theory echo this result and suggest the game being played may
be reduced to one of timing.
Determining when to exit is always difficult as these excess returns may be driven by a few big winners
which can be easy to exit prematurly, and just as easy to wait too long with a passive strategy.

A Note on Dividend Growth

To round off our analysis of Peter Lynch's One Up on Wall Street we must take a look at the stock prices of Dividend Growth stocks.

Dividends are the most direct way for an investor to earn cash from a company
A few reasons why dividend growth may predict future stock appreciation are:
1) The company is in good health since it can afford to pay dividends
2) Management's chosen dividend strategy will likely until new management comes in
3) Wealthy conservative investors use dividends as income streams and are always researching

Some investors choose not to respect dividends in fear that the company has run out of investments opportunities for its cash flows.

Here at FMI, we consider opinions, but assess the data to determine current truth.
Let's see how companies who quarter over quarter grow Dividends stack up compared to other stocks

Keeping with Lynch's "Small Stock" suggestion, we observe the SnP 600 which has an average stock price of $38.

Below we has the top 50 Dividend Growth stocks in the Snp600.

The average stock price is $56 a near 50% premium to the market.
